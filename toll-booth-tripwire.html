A dispatch in seven movements

The Toll Booth
and the Tripwire

Trump's Hormuz blockade, China's sovereignty problem, and the quiet fracture of the Western alliance — all visible within twenty-four hours of the first announcement.

Published April 14, 2026
Reading time ~28 minutes
Filed under Geopolitics
Companion to №01 — The One-Year Threshold

At 10:00 a.m. Eastern time on Monday, April 13, 2026, the United States Navy began enforcing a blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. The announcement had come the previous day from President Trump on Truth Social, in the aftermath of 21 hours of failed peace negotiations in Islamabad between Vice President JD Vance's delegation and Iranian representatives. "Effective immediately," Trump wrote, "the United States Navy, the Finest in the World, will begin the process of BLOCKADING any and all Ships trying to enter, or leave, the Strait of Hormuz." He added that the Navy would "seek and interdict every vessel in International Waters that has paid a toll to Iran."

Within hours, two things happened that almost nobody has reported together.

The first is that U.S. Central Command issued a clarifying statement that quietly narrowed the scope of Trump's declaration. CENTCOM said the blockade would apply only to vessels entering or leaving Iranian ports on the Arabian Gulf and Gulf of Oman. U.S. forces, CENTCOM specified, "will not impede freedom of navigation for vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz to and from non-Iranian ports." The "all or none" blockade Trump had promised on Fox News was, within twenty-four hours, being quietly renegotiated into a targeted interdiction operation against a much smaller set of ships than his rhetoric implied.

The second is that the United Kingdom publicly refused to participate. A UK government spokesperson told CBS News: "The Strait of Hormuz must not be subject to tolling. We are urgently working with France and other partners to put together a wide coalition to protect freedom of navigation." A senior NATO military official confirmed to the same outlet that the UK is leading planning efforts for a coalition of more than forty nations to reopen the strait — separate from, and in implicit tension with, the U.S. blockade.

These two developments are the real story of the week. The blockade is already smaller than it was announced to be, and the coalition that was supposed to enforce it is already forming against it. The question is not whether Trump's blockade will achieve its stated goals. The question is whether it survives contact with the two adversaries it is actually colliding with — and one of those adversaries is not Iran.

Chapter I

What the Blockade Actually Is

Begin with the gap between the announcement and the operation, because that gap is where the dispatch's central insight lives.

Trump's Sunday declaration was maximalist. "All or none." Every ship. Every vessel that had paid a toll. The Navy, he said, would "seek and interdict every vessel in International Waters that has paid a toll to Iran," and he threatened that "any Iranian who fires at us, or at peaceful vessels, will be BLOWN TO HELL." Taken literally, this was a declaration that the United States intended to exercise sovereign maritime authority over the entire Strait of Hormuz and the international waters around it — an authority that, under customary international law and the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea, the United States does not possess and has never claimed.

CENTCOM's Monday clarification was an acknowledgment of that legal reality. The blockade, as actually being enforced, applies to vessels entering or departing Iranian ports. Freedom of navigation through the strait itself — the right of commercial vessels to transit between non-Iranian destinations — is explicitly preserved. This is not a blockade in the nineteenth-century sense, where a naval power closes a waterway to all traffic. It is a targeted interdiction operation against a specific subset of shipping: vessels with Iran as origin or destination, plus vessels that the United States assesses to have paid tolls to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps.

Why does this distinction matter? Because it determines who the operation actually collides with.

A blockade that stopped every ship in the strait would primarily harm China, India, South Korea, and Japan — the four countries that collectively receive the overwhelming majority of Hormuz oil flows. The United States would be largely untouched, because it imports roughly two percent of its petroleum through the strait, relying instead on domestic production and supplies from Canada and Mexico. CENTCOM's narrower framing was necessary precisely because the maximalist version would have been an economic attack on American allies and strategic competitors alike, with no plausible justification under international law.

But the narrower version has its own problem, which is worse and more specific. By announcing that the U.S. Navy will interdict vessels that have paid tolls to Iran, Trump has committed the United States to a policy of boarding or stopping commercial ships in international waters based on their payment histories. And the data on who has been paying those tolls is already public.

Lloyd's List Intelligence has reported that at least two vessels transiting the strait recently paid Iranian tolls in Chinese yuan to guarantee safe passage. Chinese, Indian, and Pakistani ships have been among the few vessels moving through the strait under negotiated arrangements with Tehran. The toll system itself — Iran's de facto imposition of a charge of up to two million dollars per ship for safe passage through what was once an international waterway — has become a de facto revenue stream for the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps and a de facto accommodation by the countries most dependent on the strait.

The blockade, in its narrower CENTCOM-articulated form, is designed to shut that accommodation down. And the accommodation's biggest customer is China.

Chapter II

The Toll Booth

To understand what Trump's blockade is actually doing, you have to understand what Iran has been doing for the past six weeks.

The Strait of Hormuz has not been closed during the war. This is one of the most persistently misunderstood facts of the entire conflict. From the outside, "Iran is blockading the strait" has become a shorthand for "the strait is shut." It isn't. The strait has been open the entire time — just not freely. Iran has been operating what amounts to a toll booth, charging up to two million dollars per tanker for safe passage, and Iran has continued to export its own oil throughout the war at a rate of roughly 1.85 million barrels per day through March — about 100,000 barrels per day higher than its pre-war baseline.

This is the detail that reframes everything. The toll booth is a revenue mechanism, not a siege weapon. It funnels hard currency to the Iranian state during a war that has devastated its conventional economy and its oil infrastructure. It allows Tehran to punish some countries while accommodating others, turning the strait into an instrument of selective diplomacy rather than a blunt closure. And it has been working. The countries willing to pay — or to facilitate payment through intermediaries — have continued to receive oil. The countries unwilling or unable to pay have not.

The strait is not closed. It is priced. And the toll booth is funding the very state the United States is trying to strangle — which is why Trump's blockade is a revenue interdiction dressed up as a freedom-of-navigation operation. — The underlying mechanic

Trump's blockade is an attempt to shut the toll booth down. By committing to interdict vessels that have paid tolls to Iran, the United States is trying to cut off the revenue stream that has sustained Iran through the war — the 45% of Iran's government budget that, in peacetime, comes from Chinese oil purchases, and the cash payments from intermediaries that have kept those purchases flowing during the war itself. This is a rational strategic objective. Strangling an adversary's wartime revenue stream is a legitimate goal, and there is no question that cutting off the toll booth would increase pressure on the Iranian regime at a moment when the negotiating position matters.

The problem is that the toll booth has customers. And one of those customers is the second-largest economy in the world, a nuclear-armed permanent member of the UN Security Council, whose leadership has already publicly opposed the blockade and whose ships are the ones in the payment records.

Trump has, in effect, announced a policy of boarding Chinese commercial vessels in international waters based on financial documentation that the Chinese government considers a legitimate commercial arrangement with a sovereign state. The fact that the United States considers that arrangement illegitimate is not, under international law, a basis for interdiction. It is a basis for sanctions, diplomatic protest, and secondary enforcement through the financial system — all of which the United States has already tried. The blockade escalates from those tools to something the United States has not done to a peer competitor since the Cold War: direct physical interference with a rival great power's commercial shipping.

Chapter III

The Tripwire

Here is the scenario analysts are gaming out, and here is why it is the most dangerous aspect of the entire blockade.

Somewhere in the Gulf of Oman in the coming weeks, a Chinese-flagged commercial tanker will attempt to transit to or from an Iranian port. The vessel may be carrying crude oil, it may be carrying refined products, it may be carrying nothing at all and simply moving into position. It will have payment records, or will be suspected of having payment records, indicating that it has paid tolls to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps in yuan. A U.S. Navy destroyer operating under CENTCOM's blockade enforcement order will intercept it. A boarding team will be prepared.

At this point, the decision tree for both sides becomes extraordinarily compressed.

For the U.S. captain, the orders are clear: enforce the blockade, prevent the vessel from completing its voyage, seize it if necessary. For the Chinese captain, the orders are also clear, though they have not been made public: do not voluntarily submit to boarding by a foreign navy in international waters. Beijing has consistently held, going back decades, that the boarding of Chinese commercial vessels by foreign warships is an affront to sovereignty that must be resisted. This is not a matter of oil. China has enough oil. It has 120 days of strategic reserves, rerouted Russian pipeline supplies, domestic production covering roughly 27% of its consumption, and the technical capacity to weather a multi-month Hormuz disruption if it had to. The Columbia Center on Global Energy Policy assessed in February that China is "well-prepared to weather a multi-month disruption of its crude oil supplies from the Middle East because of its substantial oil stockpiles."

What China does not have — cannot have, politically or strategically — is the ability to accept a precedent in which the United States Navy unilaterally decides which Chinese commercial vessels are permitted to carry which cargoes in international waters. The oil is replaceable. The principle is not.

This is why i think my thesis about the sovereignty problem is the right framing, and why the familiar framing about China's energy dependence is a distraction. The real collision is not between two economies that need the oil. It is between two state systems whose operating definitions of sovereignty are mutually exclusive at the moment of interdiction.

◆ The structural collision

China does not need to force its oil through Hormuz. It has the buffers to absorb the disruption. What it cannot accept is a precedent in which Washington decides, unilaterally and in international waters, which Chinese commercial vessels are permitted to carry which cargoes. This is not an oil problem. It is a sovereignty problem, and it is the kind of problem that resolves either by one side backing down — which neither Trump nor Xi can politically afford — or by an incident neither side intended.

Consider the geometry of the encounter. A U.S. Arleigh Burke destroyer closes to within roughly a thousand yards of a Chinese tanker and its escort. The U.S. vessel launches a rigid-hulled inflatable boat carrying a boarding team. The Chinese escort frigate — and China has been building these warships specifically to escort commercial traffic through contested waters — maneuvers between the destroyer and the tanker. Now you have two warships from rival nuclear-armed great powers operating at knife-fighting range, with radars and fire-control systems active on both sides, during an active enforcement operation against the tanker that the Chinese vessel is protecting.

Every military professional who has gamed this scenario knows what the hinge point is. It is not the boarding itself. It is the three-second interval after any unexpected noise, motion, or signal in the contact zone — the window in which an automated defense system on either ship detects what it interprets as a hostile act and queries the bridge for a response. In that three seconds, a junior officer's reflex becomes a foreign policy decision, and the foreign policy decision becomes irreversible. The question of who shot first becomes the question of whose fire-control computer updated its threat board first, and the answer becomes the beginning of a war that nobody in either capital wanted.

This is not a prediction. It is a description of the scenario that the current enforcement posture has made possible. The scenario does not become reality unless specific things happen — a Chinese tanker actually attempts transit, a U.S. boarding party is actually deployed, a Chinese escort actually closes to tactical range — and each of those is a decision that can be prevented. What the blockade does is make each decision easier to reach and harder to reverse. It is a tripwire being laid, not a trap being sprung.

The question of whether the tripwire fires is the question of whether either side, in the coming weeks, finds a way to back down without publicly appearing to. It is an open question.

Chapter IV

The Quiet Fracture

While Washington has been focused on the China dimension, the most immediate challenge to the blockade has emerged from an unexpected direction. The United Kingdom has refused to participate. France is building a separate coalition. The transatlantic alliance, in its strongest form since the Second World War, is splitting in public over the Hormuz enforcement posture — within twenty-four hours of the first announcement.

The facts, as reported by CBS News: a UK government spokesperson confirmed on Monday that Britain will not join the U.S. blockade. The statement was unambiguous. "The Strait of Hormuz must not be subject to tolling. We are urgently working with France and other partners to put together a wide coalition to protect freedom of navigation." A senior NATO military official, speaking to the same outlet, confirmed that the UK is leading planning efforts for a coalition comprising more than forty nations — many of them NATO members — to reopen the strait and protect freedom of navigation, including the pre-positioning of assets.

Read that again. Within twenty-four hours of Trump announcing a unilateral U.S. naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, a NATO ally announced it was assembling a competing coalition of forty nations with a different stated objective, operating in the same waters, and pre-positioning military assets for deployment. This is not a minor diplomatic disagreement. It is not a quiet allied reluctance to participate. It is the largest public break between the United States and its closest ally over a maritime enforcement operation since the Suez Crisis of 1956.

The phrasing of the British statement is worth attending to, because every word was chosen. "The Strait of Hormuz must not be subject to tolling." The sentence names the core problem — the Iranian toll booth — and in doing so establishes that the UK agrees with the American diagnosis of what is wrong. What the UK does not agree with is the prescription. "Freedom of navigation" is not a synonym for "blockade." It is the opposite. A freedom-of-navigation coalition would, by definition, be designed to keep the strait open to all commercial traffic — including the traffic that Trump has announced the U.S. Navy will interdict.

◆ The largest break since Suez

Within twenty-four hours of Trump announcing the blockade, the UK refused to participate and began assembling a separate forty-nation coalition whose stated purpose — "freedom of navigation" — is the direct opposite of what a blockade is. This is the largest public break between Washington and its closest ally over a maritime enforcement operation in seventy years.

Why are the allies doing this? The answer is partly about law and partly about precedent and partly about the strategic math.

On the law: a blockade of an international strait is a legally fraught operation even when it targets a specific belligerent, and CENTCOM's narrower framing — targeting vessels to and from Iranian ports — reflects an attempt to keep the operation within the bounds of what the United States can defend in international forums. But "interdicting vessels that have paid tolls to Iran" in international waters is a more aggressive claim than CENTCOM's language acknowledges, and it is the kind of claim that creates uncomfortable precedents for every other maritime power. If Washington can board commercial vessels in international waters based on their financial histories, every other navy can eventually claim the same authority. The United Kingdom — a maritime power whose entire strategic tradition is built on freedom of the seas — has obvious reasons to object to the precedent even if it agrees with the immediate target.

On the precedent: Europe has watched the Trump administration spend three months openly questioning the value of NATO, demanding increased European defense spending, and conducting bilateral negotiations with adversaries that bypass allied input. A blockade of the world's most important oil chokepoint, announced on Truth Social without prior consultation with Britain or France, and immediately narrowed by CENTCOM in a way that suggests the enforcement was not fully planned before it was announced, is exactly the kind of operation that European capitals have been anticipating and preparing contingencies for. The UK-led coalition is not a response to the blockade. It is the execution of a plan that has been sitting in drawers in Whitehall and the Élysée since January.

On the strategic math: the UK and France are more exposed to a Hormuz disruption than the United States is, by an order of magnitude. Britain's refineries, Europe's energy prices, and the supply chains of every European manufacturer depend on flows through the strait to a degree that American domestic production has made largely irrelevant to U.S. economic stability. When Trump said the United States "doesn't need" the Strait of Hormuz, he was approximately correct for the United States and approximately suicidal for the European economies. The forty-nation coalition is, in part, an insurance policy against the possibility that Trump's blockade makes the strait less usable rather than more.

The ultimate question is whether the UK-led coalition actually deploys, and whether it operates in the same waters as the U.S. blockade, and what happens if a British or French warship encounters a U.S. Navy vessel enforcing an interdiction that the British or French vessel is explicitly there to prevent. None of these questions have answers yet. All of them will have answers within weeks.

The blockade has produced, in under forty-eight hours, a collision course between the United States and two distinct sets of actors: the Chinese sovereignty problem, and the Western alliance cohesion problem. Trump's team announced the blockade to pressure Iran. It may turn out that the pressure has been applied to the wrong targets.

Chapter V

What China Actually Does

Predictions about China's response should be held carefully, because the distance between Beijing's public rhetoric and its actual decision-making has been, historically, significant. But the range of plausible responses is narrower than it might appear, and the data supports a specific forecast.

China will probably not attempt to force a military confrontation in the strait. The reasons are not reasons of weakness — they are reasons of strategic patience. China has been preparing for exactly this kind of squeeze for over a decade. Its 120-day crude reserves are the largest in the world. Its pipeline imports from Russia have more than doubled in the past year. Its domestic production covers a meaningful fraction of its consumption. Its strategic investment in Saudi Arabia and the UAE has created alternative relationships with the Gulf that give it leverage over any regional settlement. And its long-term energy strategy — electrification of transportation, expansion of renewables, nuclear and hydro buildout — has been reducing its marginal dependence on seaborne oil every year for the past decade.

A military confrontation in the strait would cost China more than the oil is worth to it, and Beijing knows this. The much more likely path is a combination of three moves.

First, diplomatic acceleration. China has already been working through back channels to broker a ceasefire and a more durable settlement. The blockade increases Beijing's incentive to push harder on that track, because every day the blockade operates is a day in which the sovereignty collision becomes more visible and harder to defer. Expect Chinese diplomatic activity to intensify in Moscow, Tehran, Riyadh, and Abu Dhabi over the coming weeks. Expect Chinese offers of economic inducements — frozen assets, investment commitments, technology transfers — to any regional party willing to help construct an off-ramp.

Second, gray-zone escalation. The space between diplomatic activity and military confrontation is large, and it is where great powers have increasingly been doing their actual contesting for the past decade. Cyber operations against U.S. financial and logistics networks. Pressure on American allies through trade and technology. Rare earth export restrictions. Quiet coordination with other actors who have an interest in seeing the blockade fail — Russia, Iran, possibly some Gulf states. The goal is not to break the blockade militarily but to raise its cost to a level where Washington finds its own off-ramp. This is the playbook China has developed and refined since the 2018 trade war, and it is the playbook the Trump administration has been preparing for without having prepared for it in this specific form.

Third, and most importantly, the yuan-denominated toll continues. Chinese vessels will not stop transiting the strait. They will continue paying tolls to the IRGC in yuan, because the alternative is either to submit to U.S. interdiction authority or to abandon a policy position that Beijing cannot abandon without loss of face. The question is whether the vessels transit with Chinese naval escort, and if so, how close the escort comes, and whether the U.S. Navy attempts to enforce the interdiction against an escorted vessel. This is the tripwire from Chapter III, and its activation is a decision China is going to make in the coming days.

The smartest read of Beijing's options is that it will do all three of these things simultaneously — diplomatic acceleration, gray-zone pressure, and continued yuan-denominated transit — and will escalate or de-escalate the third track based on how Washington responds to the first two. If the U.S. blockade holds to its CENTCOM-narrowed scope and avoids actually boarding Chinese-flagged vessels, Beijing will accept the friction as a cost of doing business and work the diplomatic tracks. If a boarding is actually attempted, and especially if a Chinese crew resists, the calculation changes in ways that are genuinely unpredictable — and the tripwire problem becomes the dispatch's primary concern.

Chapter VI

How This Ends

Modern wars rarely end in Cannae-style decisive victories. They end in what strategists call convergence of expectations — the point at which both sides realize that the cost of continued friction exceeds any plausible gain from continuing to apply it. The result is almost never peace. It is a managed pause, an imperfect equilibrium, a fragile ceasefire dressed up as a settlement. Korea in 1953. Iran-Iraq in 1988. Syria, endlessly. Ukraine, now entering its fifth year of negotiated unreality. The blockade of Hormuz, when it ends, will end this way.

The question is which version of the managed pause we reach, and how much damage is done in getting there.

The favorable version — call it Path A — involves the following sequence: The UK-France coalition forms and deploys within weeks. Its presence in the strait creates a legal and operational alternative to the U.S. blockade, allowing Washington to quietly de-escalate without appearing to back down. Behind the scenes, a new round of negotiations in a less contentious venue — Muscat, Doha, possibly Geneva — produces a framework in which Iran commits to ending the toll booth in exchange for partial sanctions relief and a modified ceasefire architecture. China gets the oil flowing through a rebuilt normalization mechanism, Europe gets the freedom of navigation it was demanding, the United States gets to claim that its pressure produced the Iranian concession, and Iran gets to claim that it protected its sovereignty against American aggression. Everyone lies about who won. Nobody is happy. The system holds.

The unfavorable version — call it Path B — involves a tripwire event. A boarding that goes wrong. An escort that comes too close. A fire-control system that updates too fast. A junior officer whose reflex exceeds their authority. The collision becomes kinetic before either government can intervene, and the decision cycle compresses to minutes. Whatever happens in those minutes determines whether the incident becomes a containable crisis — apologies, walk-backs, the usual great-power choreography for averting the actual escalation — or whether it becomes the moment the United States and China discover they have no more slack in their relationship to absorb even a small unexpected shock.

The favorable version of this ending requires that both sides find a face-saving off-ramp within weeks. The unfavorable version requires only a single bad decision by a single officer in a single compressed moment. The ratio of those two probabilities is the question Fieldnotes has been writing about since the first dispatch. — The window that is open

The probability of each path depends on variables that are still in motion. Will the UK-France coalition actually deploy, or will it remain a diplomatic position rather than an operational one? Will the Chinese government order explicit military escort of yuan-paying tankers, or will it maintain the current posture of commercial-only transit? Will Trump's team hold to the CENTCOM-narrowed enforcement scope, or will the president, frustrated by insufficient pressure on Iran, expand the interdiction posture in a way that guarantees a Chinese collision? Each of these is a decision made in Washington, Beijing, London, Paris, or Tehran in the coming weeks. None of them have been made yet. The future, for once, is actually open.

What the dispatch can say is this. The favorable version exists. The off-ramps are visible. The infrastructure for de-escalation — back-channel diplomacy, multilateral maritime coordination, a possible ceasefire architecture, the Gulf states' own interest in stability — is in place. None of this requires anyone to trust anyone else. It requires each side to decide, independently, that continued friction is more expensive than grudging accommodation. That decision is one that governments make when their domestic politics give them the space to make it, and not before.

Chapter VII

What This Moment Actually Is

Pull back. What has happened this week?

A war that began in February 2026 has now crossed the threshold at which duration, not intensity, becomes the variable that shapes outcomes. The first dispatch in this series argued that the difference between a short war and a long one was the difference between a recoverable shock and a structural one, and that the one-year mark was where the shock becomes a condition. We are not yet at the one-year mark. We are at the six-week mark. What the blockade announcement reveals is that the mechanisms that turn duration into structure are already engaging — the alliance politics are already fraying, the adversaries are already testing limits, the enforcement postures are already drifting away from their initial framings, the off-ramps are already narrowing.

The blockade is not a new story. It is the old story made visible. The toll booth is not a new mechanism. It is the revenue side of a war that has been running on improvised financial arrangements since its first week. The sovereignty collision with China is not a new problem. It is a dormant problem that the blockade has just moved to the foreground. The UK-France coalition is not a new alliance. It is the public emergence of a contingency plan that has been building in allied capitals since the Trump administration began.

What is new is the speed at which all of these dynamics are now interacting. A blockade announced on Sunday was enforcement-narrowed by Monday, allied-rejected by Monday afternoon, and competing-coalition-challenged by Monday evening. The compression of the decision cycle, which the entire Fieldnotes series has been arguing is the defining feature of modern systemic stress, is visible in the calendar of the last seventy-two hours.

The question every dispatch in this series has been circling is whether the institutions and individuals responsible for the global order can make decisions faster than the decisions are made for them. The blockade is the current test case. The answer will be visible within weeks.

The blockade began Monday morning. The allies began backing away by Monday afternoon. The tripwire is somewhere in the water — and the only question left is whether anyone can see it in time.

Postscript — The Through-Line

Five dispatches in, the pattern across the series has become visible enough to name.

The One-Year Threshold argued that duration, not intensity, is the variable that transforms recoverable shocks into structural ones. This dispatch is what that argument looks like at the six-week mark. The munitions are being expended. The alliances are fraying. The enforcement postures are drifting. The one-year threshold is six months closer than it was when the first dispatch published.

The Ugarit Warning argued that complex systems fail not under single shocks but under paired stresses. This dispatch is a paired-stress event in real time. The blockade is one stress. The allied fracture is a second. The Chinese sovereignty collision is a third. Any one of them is absorbable. The combination is what the Ugarit Warning was about.

The Signature Problem argued that governance, not technology, is the binding constraint on migration through predictable threats. The blockade is a governance failure — a unilateral enforcement operation announced without allied consultation, immediately narrowed by the military that has to execute it, and already contested by the allies it was meant to include. The technical tools for a more coordinated response exist. The governance structures to deploy them did not operate in time.

The Abdication Instinct argued that the alignment question for the 2030s is whether trained patterns of self-limitation survive contact with real operational stress. The blockade is that stress, applied to humans rather than machines. The system designed to prevent unilateral escalation — allied consultation, multilateral enforcement, legal constraints, diplomatic channels — has been bypassed or narrowed or ignored in the course of three days. Whether the trained institutional instinct toward coordinated action survives this test is, as of this morning, genuinely unclear.

The dispatches were not planned as a series. They became one because the questions kept pointing at the same structural concern. Complex systems fail not when the threat arrives but when the threat arrives faster than the institutions can process it. The blockade has produced a threat that is moving very fast. The institutions designed to absorb it are visibly lagging. The tripwire is in the water. Whether it fires depends on decisions that are being made right now, by people most readers will never meet, in rooms they will never see.

We are living in the gap between the announcement and the outcome. That gap is the entire story, and it is the story Fieldnotes has been writing about from the beginning. This is where it gets tested.
