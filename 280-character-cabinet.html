A dispatch in seven movements

The 280-Character
Cabinet

How Trump, Iran, and the new memetic statecraft are running ahead of the institutions designed to constrain them — and why the institutions are losing the contest.

Published April 15, 2026
Reading time ~26 minutes
Filed under Politics / Media
Companion to №05 — The Toll Booth and the Tripwire

Three things happened on Monday, April 13, 2026, that almost no commentator has reported together.

At 10:00 a.m. Eastern time, the United States Navy began enforcing the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz that President Trump had announced on Truth Social the previous morning. By midmorning, the White House communications staff was scrambling to delete an AI-generated image, posted by Trump on Truth Social shortly after the blockade announcement, depicting the president as Jesus Christ healing a sick man — a post that had remained on his account for roughly thirteen hours and had drawn condemnation not from the political left but from his own Christian base. By Monday afternoon, Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei was at a press conference in Tehran condemning YouTube for having banned an Iranian production company called Explosive Media, whose AI-generated Lego-style animations mocking Trump and Netanyahu had accumulated tens of millions of views in the weeks since the war began.

One story is about a naval enforcement operation. One story is about a blasphemous deepfake. One story is about a propaganda video account being suspended from YouTube. They look like three different stories. They are the same story.

The same story is this: state communication has migrated from formal institutional channels to platform-mediated memetic content, and the institutions designed to deliberate on, constrain, or implement state policy cannot keep up with the speed at which the new communication operates. The blockade was announced as a Truth Social post hours before CENTCOM had a plan to enforce it. The blasphemous image stayed up for thirteen hours because no functioning press apparatus existed between the president and the publish button. The Iranian Lego operation has out-competed every Western government's official communications channel on the metric that actually matters in the platform environment, which is not accuracy or authority but virality.

Chapter I

The Sunday Night Post

Begin with the Jesus image, because the Jesus image is the cleanest possible illustration of how the temporal-mismatch problem operates inside a head of state.

Late on Sunday, April 12 — the same evening Trump announced the Hormuz blockade — the president posted to Truth Social an AI-generated image depicting himself in white robes and a red sash, one hand emitting a ball of light and the other extended in healing toward a sick man in a hospital bed, with U.S. flags, bald eagles, the Statue of Liberty, and fighter jets arranged in the background. The image was a slightly altered version of one originally circulated months earlier by Nick Adams, a right-wing influencer Trump had appointed in March as special presidential envoy for American tourism, exceptionalism and values. The original Adams post had been captioned: "America has been sick for a long time. President Trump is healing this nation."

The image stayed on Trump's account for approximately thirteen hours.

The backlash, when it came, came not from Trump's political opponents but from his own coalition. Riley Gaines, a conservative activist and staunch Trump ally, posted that she "cannot understand" why Trump would share the image. Erick Erickson wrote that the media should be paying attention to "the Christian Trump supporters who have stood with him through Iran, who are waking up to his blasphemy." Conservative commentator Allie Beth Stuckey said the image was what happens "when Paula White is your personal pastor and people around you are continually comparing you to Christ." Bishop Robert Barron of Winona-Rochester, a member of Trump's own Religious Liberty Commission, called the post "entirely inappropriate and disrespectful" and said the president owed the Pope an apology. Senator Tim Scott, the only Black Republican in the Senate, had already publicly criticized an earlier Trump meme involving the Obamas. Marjorie Taylor Greene, who has become increasingly distant from the administration, wrote on X: "On Orthodox Easter, President Trump attacked the Pope because the Pope is rightly against Trump's war."

The image was deleted Monday morning. Trump held an unscheduled press conference outside the Oval Office Monday afternoon to explain it. His explanation, delivered to reporters, was that he thought the image depicted him as a Red Cross doctor. "I did post it, and I thought it was me as a doctor," he said. "And it had to do with Red Cross. There's a Red Cross worker there, which we support. And only the fake news could come up with that one." He continued: "I just heard about it, and I said, 'How did they come up with that?' It's supposed to be me as a doctor, making people better, and I do make people better, and make people a lot better."

This explanation, which would not survive even a casual examination of the image — the figure is wearing biblical robes, not scrubs; there is no Red Cross insignia visible; the surrounding iconography is patriotic rather than medical — became its own news cycle. Jon Stewart spent a Daily Show segment on it. The deletion did not end the story. The deletion was the story, because the deletion exposed the absence of any institutional apparatus capable of preventing the post from happening in the first place.

The image stayed up for thirteen hours. The press secretary did not stop it. The chief of staff did not stop it. The religious-outreach apparatus did not stop it. The deliberative machinery of the executive branch did not stop it. There is no machinery. There is only the publish button and the man with his thumb on it. — The institutional gap, made visible

This is not the first time the apparatus has failed. On February 5 of this year, Trump's account posted a video that ended with an AI-generated clip of Barack and Michelle Obama's faces superimposed on the bodies of apes dancing to "The Lion Sleeps Tonight." That post stayed up for nearly twelve hours. The White House initially defended it through press secretary Karoline Leavitt, who told reporters: "This is from an internet meme video depicting President Trump as the King of the Jungle and Democrats as characters from the Lion King. Please stop the fake outrage and report on something today that actually matters to the American public." The post was eventually removed after Senator Tim Scott called it "the most racist thing I've seen out of this White House" and Senators Pete Ricketts and Roger Wicker publicly broke with the president. The White House then pivoted to blaming a staffer for posting it "erroneously." Trump, speaking aboard Air Force One that evening, declined to apologize and acknowledged he had personally directed the repost.

A pattern is visible across these incidents that is more interesting than any single one of them. The president posts something. The institutional apparatus around him does not catch it before publication, because there is no apparatus that operates at the speed of his posting. The post generates backlash. The press secretary defends it for several hours. The president eventually deletes it. A face-saving explanation is offered that does not survive examination. The cycle repeats.

Each individual incident is a story about Donald Trump. The pattern across the incidents is a story about something larger.

Chapter II

The Lego War

While the White House has been losing the same fight with the publish button repeatedly, a small group of Iranian content producers have been winning a different kind of fight. The contrast between the two operations — between the world's most online presidency and a sanctioned authoritarian regime's English-language meme studio — is the most analytically important detail in the entire memetic statecraft story.

The studio is called Akhbar Enfejari — Persian for "Explosive News" — though it operates publicly as Explosive Media. It produces AI-generated animations in the visual style of the Lego Movie franchise, set to original rap tracks, dramatizing the U.S.-Israel war on Iran from the Iranian perspective. The videos depict Trump as old, isolated, controlled by Israeli interests, and obsessively avoiding the Epstein investigation. They reference Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's history of alcoholism. They mock Netanyahu as Trump's puppeteer. One ends with a parade of Lego caskets draped in American flags while a chorus sings "the slaughterhouse is open." Another shows Trump being literally walked like a dog by Netanyahu. A third, released after the most recent ceasefire announcement, simply declared "Iran won."

The videos have accumulated tens of millions of views across X, Instagram, TikTok, and Bluesky. They are, on the metric of viral reach during the current war, outperforming the official communications output of the United States government.

This is not an accident, and it is not the work of a small group of friends with laptops, despite the producers' insistence that they are independent. Mahsa Alimardani, a director at the human-rights organization WITNESS who has analyzed AI video evidence in conflict zones, told the Associated Press that the bandwidth and sophistication required to produce and distribute content of this quality, from inside Iran during a period of severe internet restrictions, indicates official or semi-official coordination with the regime. Darren Linvill, a researcher at Clemson University who studies state propaganda, found in a study completed within twenty-four hours of the war's outbreak that dozens of social media accounts affiliated with the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps had immediately begun posting Iranian propaganda about the war, some of which reached audiences of millions. Nancy Snow, an academic specialist on propaganda and the author of more than a dozen books on the subject, summarized the contest in a quote to the Associated Press: "Whoever controls the meme controls the mood."

◆ The asymmetry that matters

The Iranian operation is not improvised. It draws on a decades-long state propaganda apparatus that has been refining its understanding of American political culture and platform dynamics since the 1979 revolution. Western government communications offices, by contrast, are still operating with the procedures and assumptions of the press-conference era. On the metric that matters in the platform environment — viral reach during an active conflict — the apparatus that prepared for the platforms is currently winning, and the apparatus that did not prepare for them is currently losing.

The Iranian operation is not just running on a back office of student volunteers, despite the studio's denials. Top Iranian officials are personally participating. Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps spokesman Ebrahim Zolfaghari has been delivering official IRGC communications in English using Trump's own catchphrases — including a deadpan "Hey, Trump, you are fired." Parliamentary speaker Mohammad-Bagher Ghalibaf has been posting CNN articles about U.S. gas prices with captions like "It's not America First anymore — it's Israel First." President Masoud Pezeshkian, foreign ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei, and various Iranian embassies have all joined the meme war. Iran's Embassy in South Africa posted, on Wednesday of last week, a picture of the Iranian flag with the caption "Say hello to the new world superpower."

This is what a state communications apparatus looks like when it has decided to operate at platform speed. The State Department does not have an equivalent. CENTCOM does not have an equivalent. The British Foreign Office does not have an equivalent. The institutions whose job it is to produce official Western communications about the war are still operating at the tempo of the briefing, the press conference, and the carefully drafted statement, while the Iranian apparatus is producing AI-generated rap tracks that get tens of millions of views before any Western response can be drafted.

Iran is winning the meme war. This is a sentence that sounds absurd until you sit with it for a moment, and then it sounds like the truest sentence you can write about the current information environment.

Chapter III

The Pattern

Pull back from the two cases and the pattern becomes legible.

This is not a story about Trump, and it is not a story about Iran. It is a story about every modern state simultaneously discovering that platform-native communication is more effective than institutional communication, and converging on the same set of techniques regardless of ideology, regime type, or political tradition. The convergence is the argument.

Israel deployed an AI-generated video of Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu appearing to speak in Farsi early in the war, in which he urged Iranians to overthrow their government. Russia circulated fabricated Lego-style imagery ahead of Moldova's 2025 parliamentary elections, designed to stoke fears that Moldova would be dragged into the Ukraine war if it supported certain parties. China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs has institutionalized "wolf warrior diplomacy" as a memetic strategy that operates on different platforms in different languages with different rhetorical postures depending on the audience. The Houthis have used music videos to communicate maritime threats. Volodymyr Zelensky built and sustained Western support for Ukraine through a years-long memetic campaign that was as central to the war effort as any weapons delivery. ISIS pioneered the entire action-trailer aesthetic for recruitment videos a decade ago, and the techniques it developed have been adopted, refined, and operationalized by state actors who would have considered such methods beneath them five years earlier.

The White House communications office under Karoline Leavitt has been producing what Leavitt herself has described as more than two billion impressions worth of memetic content. The output draws heavily on American TV shows and sports clips, deploys AI-generated video sequences, and is aimed almost entirely at a domestic audience rather than at the international audiences that Iran's apparatus targets. This is a meaningful distinction. The Western memetic apparatus is competing for domestic political attention. The Iranian memetic apparatus is competing for global narrative control. The two operations are using the same techniques for different ends, and on the metric of cross-border virality, the second is currently winning.

The pattern crosses ideological lines without difficulty. The leaders involved have nothing in common except the recognition that platform-native communication is faster, cheaper, and more effective than institutional communication for the specific purposes they are using it for. A Kentucky-born president and the spokesman for the IRGC and the prime minister of a Mediterranean democracy and a content studio in Tehran and a wartime president in Kyiv are all running variants of the same playbook, because the playbook works in the environment they are all operating in.

The institutions designed to mediate state communication — embassies, foreign ministries, press secretaries, briefing rooms, prepared statements, the entire deliberative apparatus that took shape over the twentieth century — were built for a different environment. They were built on the assumption that the sender of an official communication had time to consider it before sending, that the receiver had time to deliberate before responding, and that both sides had access to walk-back mechanisms if a message produced unintended effects. Memetic statecraft operates on platforms that eliminate all three conditions, and the apparatus built for the slower environment cannot adapt to the faster one without losing the deliberative properties that gave it legitimacy in the first place.

This is the structural problem, and there is no version of it that resolves cleanly.

Chapter IV

The Speed Asymmetry

Here is the analytical core. Strip away the specific examples and what remains is a structural claim about how communication speed determines who controls the agenda in any system where multiple actors are operating at different tempos.

When one actor in a communications system decides to operate at platform speed while the others continue operating at institutional speed, the platform-speed actor captures the agenda. They define the framing. They force the slower actors to respond on terms they did not choose, in language that is already organized around the faster actor's preferred frame. The slower actors can either accept those terms — which means losing the deliberative properties that distinguished their communications in the first place — or they can refuse to engage at platform speed, which means ceding the agenda entirely.

There is no third option, because the platforms themselves do not slow down to accommodate institutional deliberation. The platforms reward whatever moves at platform speed, and they punish whatever does not.

This explains why so many leaders have converged on memetic statecraft despite the institutional costs. It is not a matter of ideology or temperament. It is a matter of structural advantage in a specific kind of communications environment. A leader who decides to operate at platform speed gains an advantage over every other actor in the system who is still operating at institutional speed, and the advantage compounds the longer the asymmetry persists. Trump is more visible than any other leader in the West partly because he posts faster than any other leader in the West, and the institutions that would normally mediate or constrain a head of state's public communications cannot operate at the speed of his posting.

The Iranian apparatus has figured out the same thing from the other direction. Iran cannot win a kinetic war against the United States. It cannot match American military or economic power. What it can do is operate at platform speed against an adversary whose own communications apparatus is structurally slower, and on the platform metric, it can produce content that reaches more eyes than any official Western response can match. The IRGC's communications operation is a cheap and effective asymmetric weapon, and Iran has been investing in it for years precisely because the return on the investment is high relative to the alternatives.

The temporal-mismatch frame that has run through the previous five Fieldnotes — the recognition that complex systems fail when the threat arrives faster than the institutions designed to absorb it — applies here in its purest form. The threat in this case is not external. The threat is generated by actors who have figured out that operating faster than the institutional apparatus gives them an advantage that the institutional apparatus cannot defeat. Trump is not threatening American democratic institutions from outside. He is operating at a speed that the institutions were never designed to constrain. The IRGC is not threatening Western information ecosystems from outside. It is operating at a speed that Western communications offices were never designed to compete with. The result, in both cases, is the same: the institutions designed to mediate state communication are being routed around by actors who have decided the mediation is a feature they are willing to pay the cost of doing without.

Kurt Vonnegut, in Mother Night, gave us the sentence that fits this moment better than anything from the contemporary commentariat: "We are what we pretend to be, so we must be careful about what we pretend to be." The leaders running memetic statecraft operations are pretending to be the kind of figures their memes depict — pretending to heal the sick, pretending to win the wars, pretending to bestride the platforms as masters rather than as employees. The moral hazard of memetic statecraft is not that the memes are false. It is that the institutions designed to constrain the leaders no longer have the speed required to remind them what they actually are.

Chapter V

The Numbers Behind the Pulpit

One of the things memetic statecraft is supposed to do is generate political support faster than the slower deliberative apparatus can erode it. The Trump-Pope feud, which is the political subplot running underneath the Hormuz blockade and the Jesus image, is a useful case study in why this assumption may be wrong.

Pope Leo XIV, who was elected in April 2025 as the first U.S.-born pontiff in the history of the Catholic Church, has been increasingly explicit in his opposition to the Iran war. On April 10, 2026, he posted on X: "God does not bless any conflict. Anyone who is a disciple of Christ, the Prince of Peace, is never on the side of those who once wielded the sword and today drop bombs." When Trump threatened that "a whole civilization will die tonight" if Iran failed to meet a U.S. deadline, the Pope, speaking to reporters near Rome, called the threat "truly unacceptable." On Saturday, April 11, the Pope presided over an evening prayer service at St. Peter's Basilica during which he urged "those who have the power to unleash wars" to "choose peace." The next morning, Trump used a lengthy Truth Social post to call the Pope "WEAK on Crime" and "terrible for Foreign Policy," to suggest the Pope was "hurting the Catholic Church," and to claim — extraordinarily — that "If I wasn't in the White House, Leo (XIV) wouldn't be in the Vatican." Hours later, he posted the Jesus image.

The political consequences are showing up in the polling.

An NBC News survey conducted by Hart Research Associates between February 27 and March 3, 2026, found that Pope Leo XIV holds a net favorability rating of plus thirty-four points among American voters — the highest of any figure tested in the survey. Trump's net favorability in the same poll stands at minus twelve. The forty-six-point gap between them is the defining data point of the current week. CNN's chief data analyst Harry Enten, presenting the findings on the network's News Central program on April 13, called it "a blowout." Trump beat Kamala Harris among American Catholics by twenty points in the November 2024 election, according to CNN exit polling. A March 2026 Fox News survey now shows Catholic net approval for Trump's handling of the Iran war at minus ten points, and Catholic net approval for his overall conduct toward Iran at minus twenty.

Pew Research Center tracking data cited by Axios on April 13 shows that Trump's approval among white Catholics fell from 59% in February 2025 to 52% in January 2026 — before the Iran war intensified and before the current Vatican confrontation. April 2026 polling now puts overall Catholic approval for Trump at 48%, down from the 55% he secured in the 2024 election. Sixty percent of American Catholics disapprove of Trump's handling of the war. Andrew Chesnut, who holds the Catholic studies chair at Virginia Commonwealth University, told Axios: "I cannot think of any parallels, at least coming from Western Christian majority countries, of such pointed and public attacks on the Pope."

The political risk is not abstract. Catholics are the largest swing religious bloc in American politics, making up roughly one in five voters nationally. Even a two-to-three percent shift away from Republican candidates in Pennsylvania, Michigan, and Wisconsin could flip control of the House or Senate in the November midterms. Trump is not just losing the Pope feud as a matter of personal popularity. He is losing it as a matter of electoral mathematics, and the Jesus image is the kind of post that accelerates the loss rather than reversing it.

This matters for the dispatch's central argument because it shows that memetic statecraft does not, in fact, escape the political consequences it is designed to outpace. The deliberative apparatus that Trump's posting bypasses is slow, but it is still operating, and it is still producing measurable political effects on a longer timescale than the post-and-delete cycle of Truth Social. The institutions are not gone. They are simply operating at a different tempo, and the actors who route around them at platform speed do not, in the end, escape the slower judgments those institutions still produce. They merely delay them.

The memetic statecraft advantage is real on the metric of agenda-setting. It does not appear to be real on the metric of durable political outcome. Whether that gap closes in either direction is the question the next election cycle will answer.

Chapter VI

What the Institutions Cannot Do

Three things the institutional apparatus cannot do in the platform environment, and why each one matters.

It cannot move at platform speed without abandoning the deliberative procedures that give institutional communication its legitimacy. A press secretary who tweets at the speed of a presidential post has stopped being a press secretary in any meaningful sense. The job of the press secretary is to translate, to clarify, to provide context, to check facts, to consult with relevant agencies before commenting on policy — and all of those activities take time. Compressing them eliminates them. The institutions that have tried to operate at platform speed have, without exception, produced communications that are indistinguishable from the unconsidered posts of the principals they are supposed to be supporting. This is not a failure of skill. It is a structural constraint.

It cannot cede the platform environment to memetic communication without losing the agenda-setting power that has historically been its primary function. If the State Department refuses to operate on X, the conversation about American foreign policy on X happens without the State Department, and the absence of an official voice is filled by whoever posts loudest and fastest. The same is true of every other institutional actor in the communications system. Refusing to engage with the platforms is not neutrality. It is surrender of the field to actors who have no reason to refuse. The institutions are forced to participate even when participation costs them the deliberative properties that distinguished them.

It cannot find a stable middle ground, because every attempt to operate at half-speed produces communications that look slow on the platforms and unconsidered through the institutional lens. A press release issued four hours after a presidential post is too late to shape the platform conversation and too rushed to satisfy the institutional review process. A statement issued the next morning is yesterday's news on the platforms and an admission of disorganization to the institutional audience. The structural problem is that the platform tempo and the institutional tempo are incompatible, and any attempt to operate between them satisfies neither set of requirements.

◆ The institutional dilemma

The institutions cannot speed up without losing what made them institutions. They cannot stay at their current tempo without losing the platforms. They cannot find a middle ground because the middle ground does not exist. The structural problem is that the deliberative apparatus and the platform environment are organized around incompatible assumptions about time, and the gap between them is widening rather than closing.

The honest forecast is that the institutions will continue to lose ground in the platform environment until either they fundamentally restructure how they communicate — which would require giving up properties that are central to their identity — or until the platform environment itself changes in some way that nobody currently anticipates. Neither of those is likely to happen quickly. The platforms are not going to slow down. The institutions are not going to give up the deliberative procedures that justify their existence. The gap is going to keep widening, and the actors who have figured out how to operate inside the gap are going to keep gaining ground at the institutions' expense.

This is not a description of a temporary anomaly. It is a description of the new equilibrium, and the equilibrium is unstable in ways that the next several years will make visible.

Chapter VII

What Monday Was

Return to the three stories from the morning of April 13.

The blockade enforcement at 10:00 a.m. ET. The deletion of the Jesus image. The Iranian foreign ministry's condemnation of the YouTube ban. They are not three stories. They are three faces of a single underlying transformation: the migration of state communication from formal institutional channels to platform-mediated memetic content, and the corresponding inability of the institutions to keep up.

The blockade was a Truth Social post. CENTCOM spent the next twenty-four hours retroactively narrowing its scope to something the Navy could actually execute. The Jesus image was a post. The White House communications staff spent the next thirteen hours figuring out how to respond, and then the next several days defending an explanation that nobody believed. The Iranian Lego operation is not a single post but a sustained campaign, and Iran's foreign ministry was, on Monday, defending it through the kind of formal diplomatic channel that the Iranian apparatus has otherwise abandoned in favor of viral content. The institutions are still showing up to defend the consequences of decisions the institutions did not make. That is the shape of the new equilibrium.

Monday was not a turning point. Monday was the day the equilibrium became visible enough that an honest observer could no longer pretend it was a series of discrete incidents. The dispatch you are reading is an attempt to name the equilibrium clearly enough that the next few months of news will be legible as expressions of it rather than as separate stories about separate scandals.

The temporal-mismatch problem that has run through the previous five Fieldnotes — the gap between the speed of threats and the speed of institutional response — has now been demonstrated in a domain where the previous dispatches did not look. The earlier dispatches showed the problem in wars, in civilizational shocks, in cryptographic protocols, in alignment architectures, and in alliance fractures. This dispatch shows the problem in the most basic function any state institution performs, which is the production of authoritative communication about what the state is doing and why. The institutions cannot produce authoritative communication faster than the principals they are supposed to be supporting can produce unauthoritative communication, and the unauthoritative communication is winning.

Whoever controls the meme controls the mood, Nancy Snow said. The dispatch's only addition to that observation is this: whoever controls the mood controls the agenda, and whoever controls the agenda controls what the institutions are forced to spend their slower deliberative cycles responding to. The institutions are no longer setting the questions. They are answering questions set by the platform-speed actors who have figured out that asking faster is more important than asking better.

The institutions have been outrun. The question is not whether they catch up. The question is what governs in the meantime — and the answer, increasingly, is whoever can produce the most viral content before the deliberative apparatus has a chance to respond.

◆ End of dispatch

Postscript — The Through-Line

Six dispatches in, the pattern across the series is unambiguous enough to name without hedging.

The One-Year Threshold showed the temporal mismatch in military endurance — the gap between the speed at which munitions are expended and the speed at which they can be replaced. The Ugarit Warning showed it in civilizational stress — the gap between the speed at which paired shocks arrive and the speed at which institutional buffers can absorb them. The Signature Problem showed it in cryptographic governance — the gap between the speed at which the quantum threat is approaching and the speed at which the protocol governance can migrate. The Abdication Instinct showed it in machine alignment — the gap between the speed at which AI capabilities are advancing and the speed at which the institutions designed to constrain them can be tested. The Toll Booth and the Tripwire showed it in alliance politics — the gap between the speed at which a unilateral blockade can be announced and the speed at which the allies designed to coordinate with the United States can object.

The 280-Character Cabinet shows it in the most fundamental layer of the system, which is the production of state communication itself. Every other dispatch in the series has assumed that the institutional apparatus, however slow, was at least operational. This dispatch argues that the apparatus is being routed around at the level of its most basic function, and that the routing has become so normalized that the institutions are now showing up primarily to clean up after decisions they did not make and could not have prevented.

Six cases. One pattern. The pattern is this: complex systems fail not when the threat arrives but when the threat arrives faster than the institutions can process it, and increasingly, the threats are being generated by actors who have figured out that operating faster than the institutions gives them an advantage the institutions cannot defeat from inside their own procedures.

The dispatches were not planned as a series. They became one because the questions kept pointing at the same structural concern. We are living in the gap between the announcement and the outcome, and the gap is the entire story. The institutions are not gone. They are simply slower than the events they are supposed to govern, and the actors who have noticed this are running ahead of them faster every week.
